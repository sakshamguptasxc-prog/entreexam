Unit 1 · 16 Lectures
Introduction to Entrepreneurship
Meaning, scope, characteristics, classifications, creativity, innovation, and entrepreneurship in diverse contexts.
1.1 Meaning of Entrepreneurship
▼

Entrepreneurship is the process of identifying an opportunity, mobilising resources, assuming calculated risk, and creating value through the establishment or expansion of a business venture. It is a dynamic economic and managerial function through which an individual or group introduces innovation, organizes production, addresses market gaps, and pursues sustainable growth.

An entrepreneur is not only a business owner but also an innovator, decision-maker, risk-bearer, and change agent. Entrepreneurship involves vision, initiative, creativity, leadership, and the ability to convert uncertainty into an organised commercial or social opportunity.

1.2 Scope of Entrepreneurship
▼

The scope of entrepreneurship is very wide — extending across sectors, scales, technologies, ownership patterns, and developmental contexts. Major dimensions include:

  • Business Creation & New Venture Development — Ideation, feasibility analysis, resource mobilisation, scaling. Example: A Kolkata boutique bakery for health-conscious consumers.
  • Innovation & Product/Service Development — New products or processes to improve customer value. Example: Zerodha transformed stock trading with low-cost digital brokerage.
  • Employment Generation & Self-Employment — Absorbs labour; creates direct and indirect jobs.
  • Rural & Agricultural Development — Agri-business, food processing, dairy, handicrafts, rural tourism. Example: Mango pulp processing unit in Malda.
  • Social & Developmental Problem-Solving — Social entrepreneurship addressing education, sanitation, healthcare, inclusion. Example: SELCO India — sustainable energy for underserved communities.
  • Digital, Global & Scalable Opportunities — E-commerce, SaaS, online education. Local ventures scaling globally.
1.3 Importance of Entrepreneurship
▼
  • Promotes Economic Growth — Contributes to national income, productivity, market expansion. Startup ecosystems in Bengaluru, Hyderabad, Pune drive India's growth.
  • Generates Employment — Food delivery startups create jobs for delivery partners, developers, support teams.
  • Encourages Innovation & Competitiveness — UPI-based fintech forced traditional banks to upgrade digital systems.
  • Utilises Resources Efficiently — Converts underutilised resources (agricultural waste → eco-friendly packaging).
  • Improves Standard of Living — Affordable edtech platforms bring quality coaching to smaller towns.
  • Supports Balanced Regional Development — MSME clusters in Nadia, Murshidabad, Birbhum reduce migration pressure.
1.4 Entrepreneurial Functions
▼
Opportunity Identification
Scanning market, understanding unmet needs, spotting gaps convertible into ventures.
Idea Generation & Innovation
Developing new products, services, or business models — entirely new or improved.
Risk-Bearing
Assuming business risk — possibility of loss from market changes, competition, or technology shifts.
Decision-Making
Crucial decisions on product, location, pricing, funding, staffing, marketing, and expansion.
Organising Resources
Bringing together land, labour, capital, technology, and raw materials.
Planning & Goal Setting
Setting objectives, business planning, forecasting demand, budgeting, and defining growth strategies.

1.9 Creative Process — 5 Stages
▼
Stage 1
Idea Germination
A seed-thought arises — vague sensing of a problem or opportunity. Raw initial impression.
Stage 2
Preparation
Active effort: gathering information, observing needs, researching. Idea takes clearer shape.
Stage 3
Incubation
Subconscious processing. Ideas develop internally even when not actively thinking about the problem.
Stage 4
Illumination
The "aha" moment — breakthrough insight. The idea appears workable and confidence grows.
Stage 5
Verification
Idea is tested, evaluated, and validated. Feasibility, viability, and commercial acceptability checked.
Breakfast service example: Germination = noticing office-goers want healthy breakfast but have no time. Preparation = researching customer preferences, pricing, demand. Incubation = stepping back; subscription-box idea emerges naturally. Illumination = cloud kitchen + app-based subscription model. Verification = sample menus, cost testing, trial in one locality.
1.10 Clarence Danhof's Classification of Entrepreneurs
▼

Clarence Danhof classified entrepreneurs by their attitude towards innovation and speed of technology adoption, studying commercial farmers in the US (1820–1870). Four categories:

Innovating Entrepreneurs
True pioneers. Introduce new goods, methods, markets, or organisational forms. First movers. Highly creative, forward-looking, strong appetite for uncertainty.
Example: Developer of AI-based irrigation system for small farmers — reshaping the market, not following it.
Imitative Entrepreneurs
Adopt successful innovations introduced by others. Strong in adaptation rather than invention. Help spread innovation across regions and sectors (important in developing economies).
Example: Entrepreneur who studies app-based dairy delivery in a metro and introduces a localised version in a tier-2 city.
Fabian Entrepreneurs
Extremely cautious and sceptical about change. Late adopters — move only when failure to change threatens competitive position. Conservative, prefer stability.
Example: Traditional store owner who adopts UPI and WhatsApp orders only after all competitors have.
Drone Entrepreneurs
Most resistant to innovation. Refuse new methods even when existing system becomes unprofitable. Highly rigid, tradition-bound, ignore market signals.
Example: Manufacturer continuing outdated machinery and manual record-keeping even after repeated losses.
1.11 Intrapreneurship / Corporate Entrepreneurship (Hans Schollhammer)
▼

Intrapreneurship = entrepreneurial activity within an existing organisation. Individuals or teams inside a company behave entrepreneurially — generating new ideas, launching new products, creating new business units, or renewing existing operations.

1.13 Women in Entrepreneurship
▼

Women entrepreneurs in India own only about one-fifth of all enterprises, most of which remain micro and informal. Their participation spans retail, services, food, handicrafts, beauty & wellness, education, digital commerce, agribusiness, and social enterprises.

Major Challenges
  • Limited Access to Finance — Inadequate collateral, lower formal asset ownership, weak credit histories. Women-owned SMEs in developing countries face a major financing gap.
  • Lack of Networks & Mentorship — Fewer professional contacts, reduced access to investors and market information.
  • Technology & Market Access Constraints — Limited digital tools and technology adoption support, restricting productivity and scale.
  • Concentration in Low-profit Sectors — Retail and services typically offer lower margins than electronics, software, or construction.
  • Social & Cultural Barriers — Gender norms, mobility restrictions, time poverty, unequal care responsibilities, adverse social norms (NITI Aayog).
  • Scale & Formalisation Issues — Micro and informal ventures struggle with registration, compliance, taxation, branding, and long-term scaling.
Support Ecosystem — NITI Aayog's Women Entrepreneurship Platform (WEP)

WEP is a Government of India-backed initiative acting as an aggregator of support, providing access to: incubation & acceleration, skilling & mentorship, marketing assistance, funding support, compliance & tax assistance, and community/networking resources.

Family Business
Concept, advantages, disadvantages, founder's role, non-family members, women, succession planning, and conflict resolution.
2.1 Family Business — Concept, Advantages & Disadvantages
▼

A family business is one where a family owns a controlling interest and members of the family participate in management. It is the most common form of business organisation in India and globally. The family brings together ownership, control, and management under one roof.

Advantages
  • Strong Commitment & Loyalty — Family members are deeply committed to the business's success; they often work harder than professional managers.
  • Long-term Vision — Family businesses tend to prioritise long-term sustainability over short-term profits.
  • Trust & Low Transaction Costs — High trust among family members reduces monitoring costs and agency problems.
  • Flexibility & Quick Decision-Making — Without bureaucratic layers, decisions can be made swiftly.
  • Preservation of Values & Culture — Unique organisational culture shaped by the founder's values can become a competitive advantage.
  • Stability — Lower likelihood of hostile takeovers; family retains control.
Disadvantages
  • Nepotism & Lack of Meritocracy — Appointments may be based on family ties rather than competence.
  • Succession Problems — Unclear succession planning leads to conflict and instability.
  • Resistance to Change — Older generations may resist modernisation, professionalisation, and diversification.
  • Conflict Spillover — Personal and family disputes can disrupt business operations.
  • Limited Access to Capital — Reluctance to dilute family control can restrict external investment.
  • Role Ambiguity — Blurred boundaries between family and business roles can create confusion.
2.2 Role of Founder(s) in Family Business
▼

The founder plays a central and defining role in shaping the character, culture, and direction of a family business. The founder's values, business philosophy, and behavioural orientations become the invisible architecture of the enterprise.

  • Value Formation — The founder establishes the core values (integrity, hard work, frugality, innovation) that guide the business's ethical culture for generations.
  • Business Philosophy — The founder's approach to customers, employees, partners, and competitors shapes the organisation's identity.
  • Behavioural Orientation — Risk appetite, leadership style, and work habits of the founder become embedded norms.
  • Cultural Legacy — The founder's legacy can be both a strength (strong values, loyal culture) and a constraint (resistance to change, difficulty letting go).
The founder's reluctance to professionalise may preserve certain values but can also prevent the business from adapting to market changes. For example, a founder who micromanages may prevent the next generation from developing leadership skills.
2.3 Role of Non-Family Members in Family Business
▼

Non-family members (professional managers, independent directors, advisors) play an important and often underappreciated role in family businesses. Their involvement can be crucial to professionalisation and growth.

  • Objectivity & Neutrality — Non-family professionals can evaluate proposals on commercial grounds rather than personal preference.
  • Technical & Managerial Expertise — Professional CEOs, CFOs, or functional heads bring specialised skills that may be absent in the family.
  • Conflict Mediation — Outside advisors or board members can help mediate family disputes.
  • Credibility with External Stakeholders — Banks, investors, and partners often gain confidence when a family business has professional management.
  • Succession Bridge — Non-family managers can maintain continuity during succession transitions.
2.4 Role of Women in Family Business
▼

Women play a critical but often invisible role in family businesses. They may contribute as silent partners, operational anchors, relationship managers, or increasingly as leaders and successors.

  • Behind-the-scenes Contributors — Managing accounts, supplier relationships, daily operations, or quality control without formal titles.
  • Culture Carriers — Women often maintain the family values and culture that underpin business identity.
  • Next-generation Leaders — Increasingly, daughters are being recognised as competent successors, challenging traditional gender biases in succession.
  • Challenge: Gender Bias in Succession — In many traditional family businesses, daughters may be overlooked despite strong competence; sons may be preferred based on convention rather than merit.
2.5 Succession Planning: Choosing and Grooming Successors
▼

Succession planning refers to the process of identifying, preparing, and transitioning the leadership of a family business from one generation to the next. It is one of the most critical and challenging tasks in a family business.

Process of Choosing Successors
  • Early Identification of Potential — Assess family members' interest, aptitude, and capability from an early stage. Don't wait for crisis.
  • Merit-Based Assessment — Evaluate based on education, skills, performance, and demonstrated leadership — not birth order or affection.
  • Clear Eligibility Criteria — Define what qualifications, experience, or competencies are required for the leadership role.
  • Open Family Communication — Discuss succession openly to reduce speculation, rivalry, and resentment.
  • Gender-Inclusive Process — Daughters and other female members should receive equal consideration as potential successors.
Process of Grooming Successors
  • Education & Formal Training — Encouraging relevant degrees (business, finance, engineering) combined with practical industry exposure.
  • External Work Experience — Working outside the family business builds independent perspective, skills, and credibility.
  • Gradual Delegation of Authority — Start with smaller responsibilities; progressively assign larger decisions (product line → branch → business vertical).
  • Involvement in Strategic Decision-Making — Invite successor into discussions on expansion, financing, partnerships, and long-term planning.
  • Building Stakeholder Relationships — The successor must develop relationships with customers, suppliers, employees, bankers, and extended family stakeholders.
Common Challenges in Succession
  • Emotional bias — choosing based on affection, seniority, or tradition rather than merit.
  • Rivalry among siblings or cousins.
  • Founder's reluctance to let go — postponing succession to retain power.
  • Lack of interest among the next generation.
  • Inadequate preparation — successor chosen but not properly trained.
  • Resistance from existing employees.
  • Gender bias — daughters overlooked despite strong competence.
2.6 Conflict in Family Business & Its Resolution
▼

Conflict in a family business refers to disagreements, tension, or clashes among family members arising from differences in ownership, management, succession, control, rewards, values, or interpersonal relationships. It is both a managerial issue and a relational issue.

Major Causes of Conflict
  • Succession-related Conflict — Disagreement over who will lead the business in the future. Example: Two siblings both believing they are more suitable to succeed the founder.
  • Role Ambiguity — Unclear roles and responsibilities lead to interference. Example: A brother handling finance frustrated by another sibling's interference.
  • Unequal Contribution vs Equal Reward — Active members resent equal rewards for passive owners. Example: Daughter managing operations vs sibling not involved in business.
  • Ownership & Control Issues — Disputes over shareholding, voting power, dividend policy, or strategic control. Become more complex across generations.
  • Differences in Vision & Strategy — Older generation: conservative expansion. Younger generation: digital transformation, outside investment.
  • Personal & Emotional Rivalries — Past grievances, sibling rivalry, parental favouritism, perceived injustice.
  • Lack of Professionalism — Appointments based on family status rather than competence.
  • Entry of In-laws or Extended Family — Undue influence without clear boundaries.
  • Generational Transition — Different work styles, values, and communication expectations.
  • Communication Breakdown — Avoiding difficult issues allows resentment to build quietly over time.
Types of Conflict
TypeNatureExample
Task ConflictDisagreement over business matters — strategy, operations, budgeting. If handled well, can be constructive.Two family members disagree about whether to enter e-commerce.
Relationship ConflictEmotional tension, personality clashes, ego issues, or mistrust. Usually more damaging.Two brothers stop speaking properly due to long-term resentment.

Resolution Mechanisms
Open & Structured Communication
Periodic family business meetings. Clears assumptions, reduces suspicion, creates emotional ventilation.
Clear Role Definition
Each member has a formally assigned role with clear authority boundaries. Avoids overlap and interference.
Merit-Based Systems
Appointments, promotions, and salaries based on competence, contribution, and performance — not birth order.
Formal Succession Planning
Early identification, defined criteria, grooming plan. Reduces uncertainty and prevents rivalry.
Family Constitution / Charter
Written document recording agreed principles on ownership, employment, succession, dividend policy, dispute resolution, and family values.
Family Council / Governance
Forum to discuss family-related business issues separately from day-to-day management. Advisory boards for larger businesses.
Non-family Professionals
Neutral executives, advisors, or directors bring objectivity and reduce emotional intensity of decision-making.
External Mediation
If internal discussion fails, an external mediator (consultant, mentor, lawyer, respected elder) can resolve disputes.

3.1 Start-ups: Concept
▼

Steve Blank's definition: A start-up is "a temporary organization designed to search for a repeatable and scalable business model", explicitly contrasting it with small businesses that execute a stable, known model. Growth ambition, scalability, and uncertainty of the business model are central — not age alone.

3.3 Common Mistakes by Start-ups in Early Phases
▼
  • Premature Scaling — Trying to grow too fast before validating the business model.
  • Poor Cash Flow Management — Running out of cash despite having a good product idea.
  • Founder Conflicts — Disputes among co-founders without clear equity, role, and decision-making agreements.
  • Ignoring Customer Feedback — Building what founders think customers want rather than what they actually need.
  • Neglecting Legal & Compliance Issues — Overlooking IP protection, contracts, or regulatory requirements.
  • Hiring Mistakes — Hiring too quickly or hiring the wrong people for early-stage needs.
  • Weak Product-Market Fit — Launching without sufficient validation that the market wants the product.

Bootstrap Finance

Bootstrap finance = funding a business using only the founder's own savings, retained earnings, and revenue generated by the business itself — without relying on external investment. Advantages: full control, no dilution of equity, financial discipline. Example: Zoho grew largely through bootstrapping.

Seed Capital

Seed capital = the initial funding used to validate an idea and build an early prototype or minimum viable product. It is the earliest stage of external investment — often small amounts from angel investors, friends & family, or incubators. It helps a startup move from idea to proof-of-concept.

BasisAngel InvestorsVenture Capitalists

Stage of InvestmentSeed and early-stage (higher risk).Early growth, expansion, Series A–C.

3.7 Role of Self-Help Groups (SHGs)
▼

SHGs are small, voluntary groups — usually 10–20 members from similar socio-economic backgrounds — that pool savings, provide internal loans, and support each other's livelihoods. They are especially important for rural women's economic empowerment.

  • Financial Inclusion — Provide access to credit for members who cannot access formal banking systems.
  • Women's Empowerment — Enable women to earn independently, take financial decisions, and gain confidence.
  • Livelihood Support — Help members start or expand small enterprises (handicrafts, food processing, tailoring).
  • Social Capital Building — Create networks of mutual trust, cooperation, and collective bargaining power.
  • Linkage Banking — SHGs often get linked to formal banks (NABARD SHG-Bank Linkage Programme) to access higher credit.

Feasibility Studies & Business Plans

4.1 Concept of Feasibility Studies
▼

A feasibility study is a systematic, detailed assessment undertaken before starting a project, business, product, or expansion plan to determine whether the proposal is practical, viable, and worth pursuing. It answers the fundamental question: "Should this project be undertaken?"

It is a pre-investment appraisal tool that reduces uncertainty and helps entrepreneurs, managers, and investors avoid costly mistakes.

What a Feasibility Study Examines
  • Whether the market exists for the product/service.
  • Whether the technology is workable.
  • Whether the finances are viable.
  • Whether the organisation has the capability to execute.
  • Whether legal or regulatory barriers exist.
  • Whether the project aligns with time and risk constraints.
Importance of Feasibility Study
  • Reducing Business Risk — Identifies risks before large sums of money are committed. Example: Premium vegan café in a small town — feasibility study may reveal insufficient local demand.
  • Supporting Informed Decision-Making — Helps decide: accept, modify, postpone, or reject. Example: Online grocery delivery venture discovers strong competition → redesigns for a niche segment.
  • Estimating Resource Requirements — Capital, manpower, technology, machinery, raw materials, infrastructure, time, managerial capacity. Example: Packaged spice manufacturing unit — estimate machinery, factory, packaging, labour, branding costs.
  • Identifying Possible Constraints — Market, technical, financial, legal, environmental/social constraints. Example: Food processing unit near residential area — pollution clearance and waste disposal issues discovered early.
  • Improving Planning Quality — Refines the business model, helps choose location, finalise product mix, set pricing. Example: Cloud kitchen founder decides to begin with 2 high-demand cuisines rather than 10.
  • Building Investor Confidence — Demonstrates informed analysis beyond mere enthusiasm. Banks and VCs prefer projects with formal feasibility studies.
Types of Feasibility Study
TypeFocusKey Questions Answered
Market FeasibilityDemand, competition, target customers, pricing, market size.Is there sufficient demand? Who are the customers? What price will they pay?
Technical FeasibilityProduction processes, technology, raw materials, location, capacity.Is the product/service technically producible? Do we have the technology and capacity?
Financial FeasibilityCost estimation, funding, break-even analysis, profitability, ROI.How much capital is needed? Can the project generate adequate returns?
Organisational/Managerial FeasibilityManagement capability, human resources, structure.Does the team have the skills and capacity to execute?
Legal/Regulatory FeasibilityLicences, permits, compliance, legal constraints.Are there any legal or regulatory barriers? What approvals are required?
Environmental/Social FeasibilityEnvironmental impact, social acceptance, sustainability.Does the project comply with environmental norms? Is it socially acceptable?
4.2 Business Plan — Concept, Structure & Contents
▼

A business plan is a comprehensive written document that describes a business's objectives, strategies, market position, financial projections, and action plan for achieving its goals. It is the central document used to communicate the business idea to investors, banks, partners, and internal stakeholders.

Key Components of a Business Plan
1. Executive Summary
A brief overview of the entire business plan — business idea, USP, market opportunity, financial highlights. Written last, placed first.
2. Company Description
Business name, legal structure, location, founding date, mission statement, vision, and core values.
3. Market Analysis
Target market size, customer segments, competitor analysis, market trends, and demand forecasting.
4. Product/Service Description
Features, benefits, USP, development stage, IP protection (patents, trademarks), and R&D plans.
5. Marketing & Sales Strategy
Pricing, promotion, distribution channels, customer acquisition strategy, and sales projections.
6. Operations Plan
Location, production process, supply chain, quality control, and operational infrastructure.
7. Management Team
Founders' background, key team members, advisors, and organisational structure.
8. Financial Plan
Startup costs, revenue projections, break-even analysis, cash flow statements, P&L, and funding requirements.
9. Funding Requirements
How much funding is needed, what it will be used for, and the proposed equity/debt structure.
10. Appendices
Supporting documents — market research data, product photos, legal documents, CVs, letters of intent.
4.3 Pitch Deck for Investors — Role, Importance & Structure
▼

A pitch deck is a concise, visual presentation (typically 10–20 slides) used by entrepreneurs to present their business idea, value proposition, market opportunity, and funding requirements to potential investors. It is a distilled version of the business plan, designed for a short investor meeting (typically 10–20 minutes).

Role & Importance
  • First impression tool — secures investor interest and opens the door to further discussions.
  • Communicates complex ideas quickly and visually in a time-constrained investor setting.
  • Provides a structured narrative: problem → solution → market → business model → team → ask.
  • Forces the entrepreneur to clearly articulate their value proposition and competitive advantage.
  • Used across seed, angel, and VC funding stages.

Start-ups & Intellectual Property
IP concept, patents, copyrights, trademarks, and geographical indications — protection of intangible assets for entrepreneurs.
5.1 Concept of Intellectual Property (IP) & IPRs
▼

Intellectual Property (IP) refers to the creations of the human mind that have value and can be legally recognized as property. These arise from intellectual effort, creativity, innovation, skill, and original thinking rather than physical labour alone. Examples: inventions, literary works, artistic creations, symbols, brand names, designs, formulas, software, trade secrets.

Intellectual Property Rights (IPRs) are the legal rights granted to creators, inventors, authors, or owners of intellectual property, enabling them to control the use, exploitation, reproduction, sale, or distribution of their creations.

IP vs IPRs — Simple Distinction
Intellectual Property = the intangible creation itself (the invention, brand, design, work).
IPRs = the legal protection and exclusive rights over that creation (the shield around it).

IPRs transform creativity and innovation into legally enforceable economic assets.
What IPR Enables an Owner to Do
  • Use the creation themselves.
  • Prevent others from using it without permission.
  • License it to others (earning royalties).
  • Assign or sell the rights.
  • Earn royalties or commercial returns from the creation.
5.2 Patent
▼

A patent is an exclusive legal right granted to an inventor for a new invention. The invention may relate to a product or process offering a new technical solution to a problem or a new and useful way of doing something. In return, the inventor publicly discloses the technical details through a patent application.

Conditions for Patent Grant (3 Essential Criteria)
Novelty
The invention must be new — not previously known or publicly disclosed anywhere in the world before the filing date.
Inventive Step (Non-Obviousness)
The invention must not be obvious to a skilled person in that technical field. There must be genuine inventive merit.
Industrial Applicability
The invention must be capable of practical use or industrial application — it cannot be a mere abstract idea.
What Patent Protection Provides

Patent protection gives the owner the exclusive right to prevent others from commercially making, using, manufacturing, distributing, importing, or selling the patented invention without consent. This creates a temporary legal monopoly over commercial use of the invention.

Key Facts about Patents
AspectDetail
Duration20 years from the filing date of the application (subject to conditions and fees).
TerritorialPatent is a territorial right — valid only in countries where it is applied for and granted. An Indian patent does not automatically protect in the USA or Europe.
After ExpiryAfter the 20-year term ends, the invention enters the public domain — freely usable by anyone.
Not ProtectedMathematical methods, mental acts, abstract ideas, scientific discoveries (as such), literary/artistic works, business methods (in most jurisdictions).
Importance of Patents for Start-ups
  • Encourages R&D and rewards innovation.
  • Creates entry barriers for competitors.
  • Enables licensing and royalty income.
  • Increases firm valuation.
  • Strengthens technological competitiveness.
  • Attracts investors — a venture with patented technology appears more credible to angel investors and VCs.
Start-up with a portable solar-powered water purification device for rural households — if the filtration mechanism is genuinely new and non-obvious, they may obtain a patent. Once granted, no competitor can manufacture or sell the same invention without authorisation.
5.3 Copyright
▼

Copyright is a legal right that protects original creative works of authorship. It protects the expression of ideas, not the ideas themselves. Copyright arises automatically upon creation of the work — no formal registration is required for protection (though registration creates useful legal records).

AspectDetail
What it protectsLiterary works (books, articles, code), artistic works (paintings, photographs), musical works (compositions, recordings), films, databases, computer software.
Does not protectIdeas, concepts, facts, information, methods, systems, titles alone, slogans alone (these need trademark protection).
Duration (India)Generally the author's lifetime + 60 years after death. For films, photographs, government works: 60 years from publication.
Rights givenRight to reproduce, publish, adapt, translate, broadcast, perform, and make derivative works — exclusively.
Automatic or Registered?Arises automatically. Registration with the Copyright Office provides stronger legal evidence but is not compulsory.
5.4 Trademark
▼

A trademark is a distinctive sign, symbol, logo, word, phrase, design, sound, or combination thereof that identifies and distinguishes the goods or services of one business from those of another. It protects brand identity.

AspectDetail
What it protectsBrand names, logos, slogans, distinctive packaging (trade dress), sounds, shapes associated with a product or service.
What it does not protectGeneric or descriptive terms (e.g., "sweet" for a candy store); functional aspects of a product.
Duration (India)Registered for 10 years from the date of filing, renewable indefinitely every 10 years as long as it remains in use.
RegistrationRequires application to the Trademark Registry. Provides right to use ® symbol and legal protection against infringement.
™ vs ®™ = Unregistered trademark (claim to mark, no formal protection). ® = Registered trademark (full legal protection).
For Start-upsTrademark protects brand identity, prevents copycats, builds customer trust, and creates commercial value in the brand itself.
The brand name "Amul" and its distinctive girl logo are registered trademarks. No other company can use the Amul name or similar logo on dairy products without permission.
5.5 Geographical Indications (GI)
▼

A Geographical Indication (GI) is a sign used on products that have a specific geographical origin and possess qualities, reputation, or characteristics essentially attributable to that place of origin. GI protection is particularly important for agricultural products, handicrafts, and traditional products tied to specific regions.

AspectDetail
What it protectsProducts whose quality, reputation, or other characteristics are essentially linked to their geographical origin.
Who can use GI?Only producers/artisans located in the designated geographical region and who comply with the product specifications.
DurationRegistered for 10 years, renewable indefinitely.
Cannot be licensed or soldUnlike other IP, a GI tag belongs to the community/producers of that region — it cannot be bought, sold, or transferred to someone outside that region.
ProtectionPrevents use of the GI by parties outside the geographical area or parties that do not follow set quality standards.
Famous Indian Examples of GI-Tagged Products
Darjeeling Tea
First Indian product to receive a GI tag. Tea grown in the Darjeeling hills of West Bengal — distinctive flavour, aroma, and quality.
Basmati Rice
Long-grain aromatic rice grown in specific regions of northern India. GI protects its identity in international trade.
Kanchipuram Silk
Handloom silk sarees woven in Kanchipuram, Tamil Nadu — specific weaving technique and gold zari work.
Kolkata Rosogolla
Soft, spongy, white rosogolla originating from West Bengal — received GI tag in 2017 after a famous dispute with Odisha.
